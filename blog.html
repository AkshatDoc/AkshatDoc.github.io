Carrom: An Indian Man's Billiards

Geometry or Magic, Dec 09, 2019

So a while back(maybe 2 years), my dad and I got very competitve in a bunch of different things from how many pull-ups to chess and this specific game: carrom. As the title implies, Carrom is a game believed to be originated in the Indian Subcontient(pretty big place). It became very popular after World War I with state-level competitions held in the different states of India. By 1958, both India and Sri Lanka had formed official federations of carrom clubs, sponsoring tournaments and awarding prizes. There is even a Federation in India founded in Chennai. The game itself is pretty simple: Use your striker(big white coin) to hit the colored coins into the pockets. There are different nuances as compared to pool like: your color is predefined, you are able to hit other players coins, no penalty for missing everything etc. One of the major differences is the queen piece. This is a red piece like the regular coins but has two special restrictions. Restriction #1: Once the red coin is pocketed(scored), another "cover" coin must be scored or the queen is returned to the middle and Restriction #2: The queen must be scored before the end of the game(can't score it as your last coin). What makes the queen so special is it's point value. Each coin is worth 1 point but the queen is worht 5 points! This leads us organically to the scoring mechanics of the game. The way the scoring works seems a little strange and really differes from billiard in this way. You take the sum total of the coins left by your opponent, which becomes your points. You continue play boards(matchs) until one player reachs 29 points. Now the queen, as mentioned earlier, is worth 5 additional points so your score for a board can be expressed in an equation as : #coins_left + 5(if queen was scored by you). At this point you're probabaly wondering why I'm talking about this wacky pool like game and what it has to do with magic. Well, I recently got into the game with my father again, which then showed me how crazy the game really is.
So the board can be seen above. What makes the game so "magical " is the application of basic Geometry within it. Looking at a real game by pros, you could not believe the shots they make and how they clear the board, but when you break it down, it's just a bunch of cool triangles.
An interesting aspect I forgot to mention was the formation of the board and who gets what colour. As illustrated above, the queen is the center, with 6 coins around it(alternating color) and then 12 coins above that. Whoever is breaking must pot white coins and the other will pot the black coins. Now there are no "awesome trickshots " in this video, but just imaginge trying to hit a cylinder to another cylinder at fancy angles and different cuts to pot it in. It's not very easy(first hand experience). Some of the shots people make convince me that this game is magic like this video: .

This game is a ton of fun and I am so glad I got back into it. I just hope I don't lose my skills anytime soon and continue playing!

Dance Dance Revolution

Are School Dances Any Good?, Nov 27, 2019

Let me preface this by saying that most of my close friends are extremely introverted. Our social interactions are limited to events we volunteer at, movies and the occasional hang out. This dance was big news. In the past, I didn't want to go to these dances because of a couple of reasons(which in hindsight don't make that much sense). The first issue was the cost, which I would rationalize by saying "I can use that money elsewhere ". Did I use that money elsewhere? No, not at all because it wasn't my money I was using. The second reason was because none of my friends went, which wasn't actually true. The only friends that didn't go were those antisocial ones from before. I knew more people than I realized and on top of that, no one cares once you start dancing. All of these problems went away this year along with some self-realization. One of my friends got a girlfriend, so he was going to the "formal " dance. Along with that, a new friend of mine was running it, so he would have to be there. The cost stayed the same from previous years at a steep $60, but my great parents wanted me to try this out, which gave me the push I needed.

Oh man, this night was amazing. The beginning was a little rocky with all the photos and me arriving at a house full of just girls getting ready. The photos were rough, with the bright ring light and about 3 different cameras to look at. When we arrived at the location, things were a little better. We were talking socializing, getting acquainted with the crowd and then came the awards and dinner(both meh). When we started dancing, that was the beginning of the night. From the moshing, YMCA, jumping, singing, and all-around energy, the night could not have gone better. A small trip to McDonald's proved me wrong. I got to talk with people who I haven't talked to since grade 9 and it was really fun reconnecting with them. Overall: Dances are pretty cool. Even with the constant supervision and pat-down searches. Find some friends and tear up the night!

Charity: Donations?

The Plain Sight Robbery, Nov 26, 2019

I think I have found a new purpose for this blog. I feel like every day we have such interesting conversations with people from all around, and certain points come up that stick with you. For example, my father and I were in the car listening to the Radio when an ad about donating to homeless children. The ending to the ad was a little girl saying "don 't you care?" or something along those lines. When I heard this, I thought "oh so sad" but I guess my dad heard "oh scam" and completely agreed with me. My father believes donations are a scam because 70% of the donations go to administrations. I will further uncover the truth about this through this post.

So there were a bunch of articles that came up when I searched "where does my donation money go?". A couple of the articles seem trustworthy but as soon as I see any contradiction, we'll be in hot water(10:53 pm)
Here are the websites:
https://charity.lovetoknow.com/What_Percentage_of_Donations_Go_to_Charity, https://www.consumerreports.org/charities/best-charities-for-your-donations/, https://www.huffingtonpost.ca/phil-froats/how-much-money-goes-to-charity_b_2566538.html, https://www.spondylitis.org/Where-Does-My-Donation-Go

From reading through these 4 articles, I have found out that 17% of charity revenue comes from this holiday season alone, which makes sense since it is the time to give back. What I did find truly shocking is that a lot of good-sounding charities spend "30 cents or more for every donated dollar on things like overhead, administrative costs, and fundraising" such as The Autism Spectrum Disorder Foundation or American Printing House for the Blind. Overall, most charities do take percent to pay for "overhead" costs, such as admin, fundraisers and other necessities for the charity to grow, not just a flat 70%. As the season of jolly green and red comes around, you have to make sure that your money is going to the right place which can be achieved by searching more about the tax exemption, paying directly to the charity, Don't reply to telemarketers or mail campaigns and VOLUNTEER! (11:17)

Overall: not all charities take big cuts from your donations, but it is up to you to find out the truth about where the money goes(11:19)

Through our French class, we were exposed to the idea of "Absurdism" from Albert Camus. Using the book "L'Ã‰tranger", we were able to analyze the main character as an image of "Absurdism". Meursault has key personality traits that can be used to quntify an absurd man or "l'homme absurde"

  • Insensed
  • Indifference
  • Routine
  • Randomness
  • Determisnism

Typically Hypochondriacs are described as become unduly alarmed about any physical symptoms they detect, no matter how minor the symptom may be, and are convinced that they or others have, or are about to be diagnosed with, a serious illness. However, this definition misses a key point: the psychological aspect. After studying this book, I became more aware of my routine, my "callous" and nonchalant behavior causing me to self diagnose as absurd. Luckily, I was wrong. My "boredom" took me for a trip, making me feel absurd. My routine was the same every day at a very broad look, but each day differed in meaningful ways through friends and interactions. My "callous" behavior was not meant to cause pain or malice, but to highlight flaws in peoples views. In short, I am not a victim of circumstance nor absurd, just me.
TLDR; I became absurd because of my hypochondria (the irony)
